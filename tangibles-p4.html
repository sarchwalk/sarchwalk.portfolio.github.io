RAINBRELLA


CPSC 599.88 - Physical and Tangible HCI, Project 4

Create a wearable "fashionable" interface that communicates or reifies the concept of hygge..




Summary

Inspired by sunshowers, the RAINBRELLA seeks to connect users to nature and the feeling of well-being. The RAINBRELLA is a fashion accessory that sprays water when it's hot and sunny out, creating a sunshower for users to enjoy while staying comfortably dry.

The RAINBRELLA has the advantage of working as a regular umbrella when necessary, so that users only need to carry around one item! Aside from a bag that hangs from the handle containing the water bladder and a power supply, no other accessories are required for the RAINBRELLA to work.

To use the RAINBRELLA, users simply need to open the umbrella. The attached Arduino will use sensors to detect the current weather conditions and start the rain sequence if appropriate.

Context

To me, the feeling of hygge is strongest when I’m out in nature. This project is aimed at capturing that feeling whether you’re at home, or out exploring nature. The RAINBRELLA is designed to be used in a warm and sunny environment and creates the relaxing sound of rain, all while ensuring that you are comfortable and dry. By nature of the RAINBRELLA's size, it is intended to be used by one individual, unless another participant desires to get wet.

Physical Input





Reed Switch

The reed switch is used to detect when the umbrella is open. The switch is fixed immediately under the hood of the umbrella and a magnet is attached to one of the umbrella's arms. When the umbrella opens, the arm moves close to the reed switch, so that the magnet activates the switch.

Photoresistor

The photoresistor is used to detect whether it is sunny or cloudy out. The sensor is fixed to the outside top of the umbrella. When the umbrella is open, a value is read from the photoresistor to determine whether the conditions are good (sunny) or bad (cloudy).

Temperature and Humidity Sensor

The temperature and humidity sensor is used to determine if it is hot outside and not already raining. The sensor is fixed immediately under the hood of the umbrella, so it should not get wet when the spray is triggered.




Physical Output






Water Pump

The water pump is contained within a bag hanging on the umbrella's handle. The pump is connected to a tube that runs up the handle of the umbrella and through to the top, where a nozzle is fixed.



LED Strips

The LED strips are run along four arms of the umbrella. They turn on when the umbrella is open and spraying.



Power Supply

The pump runs off of a variable power supply set to 12v.



Prototyping Process


The Circuit



The sensors and relay were tested without the pump to ensure that they behaved as expected before being soldered to longer wires. All of the arduino code was written in this stage. The advantage of doing it this way was that I didn't have to deal with water going everywhere (yet).



The Water Bladder



I tried using a small water bladder originally used in a CPSC 581 project. However, the bladder proved to be too small, so instead I scoured my house for something larger and found a wine bag. I sawed the nozzle off and the remaining attachment fit to a make-shift adapter (the white tube) which fit to the pump's grommet.





The Nozzle


There are no action shots of this stage (due to frequent spills, slips, and overall chaos), but I tested four nozzles from an irrigation set before settling on this one.



Wiring in the RAINBRELLA


Installing the sensors, LEDs, and tubing on the umbrella was a challenge. Hot glue did not stick to the metal bar very well, so I used duct tape to attach everything. If I had the ability to build an umbrella from scratch I would have the wires and the tube run inside of the center bar, which would look cleaner than what I made.
